Anyway, I'm sure you're not reading the developer's notes for the sake of learning about me personally, so below I explain this project.

This food blog project was originally not intended for continuation or maintence. It wasn't even supposed to be food theme (but everyone likes food), but I had to find a topic in which can reflect the design of the site. Of course, it could be used as a blog about economics, sports, etc. It was orignally designed to be a universally themed blog. The only reason for this site to be food-theme was because of the images, backgrounds and the topics that were chosen.

Let's get to the building blocks of the site: I built the design and structure purely in HTML and CSS. Alongside these markup languages, I also used AngularJS to power the blogging side of things (or to be really specific, the back-end). AngularJS allows users to dynamically power their site using the Model View Controller (MVC) approach. The MVC approach follows the design principles in which functionality are sperate to design. If it weren't for AngularJS, this site would not be a blog site at all. Of course, I could've used PHP and MySQL to get posts, but AngularJS is what makes this site dynamic.

I went for a simple, elegant and minilistic design. The home page consists of three sections, the navigation, the content and the footer. These three sections are at the maximum width of 960 pixels (the go-to resolution for a lot of sites). Similarly to this page, the sections have a white background, and behind it is a simple wallpaper that is positioned statically on the view. I felt that using a white background for the whole site, especially with a small content area (of 960 pixels) would not be appealing. I also found a wallpaper that was not distracting and yet appealing and enough to constrast the white background of the contents.
